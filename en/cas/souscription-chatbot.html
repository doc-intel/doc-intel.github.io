Building a subscription chatbot: MVP first, then AI

Subscribing a product means filling a structured file. A useful chatbot starts deterministic and verifiable, before adding AI. Here is how you build it, from the simplest to the smartest.

To be clear: the demo is a basic chatbot, and we can do far better. That is precisely the point. The goal is not to impress, it is to show how you build a chatbot cleanly: a core that works, enriched afterwards, rather than an LLM API wired into everything.

The problem

A subscription is not free conversation: it is a form. You have to collect precise information (product, profile, property, level of cover, start date) in a usable format. Handing that straight to a large model that "chats" gives an unpredictable result: missed fields, badly typed values, invented answers. The risk is the same as everywhere else in the series: without structure, no reliability.

The approach: start with a deterministic core

The MVP of a good subscription chatbot needs no LLM. It is a slot-filling flow: you know the fields to fill in advance, you ask for them in order, you offer numbered choices where possible, you validate each answer, and you end with a verifiable recap and an indicative estimate. Everything is traceable and testable, without a single line of AI. That is exactly what the demo does.

Step 1 — the MVP (the demo)

A deterministic slot-filling flow

A typed schema describes the fields of the subscription. The chatbot walks them in order.

  • Choices offered as numbers (1, 2, 3…): far more reliable than free text.
  • Simple per-field validation (a postcode is five digits, a floor area is a number).
  • Estimates the sum to insure (the home contents value) from a deterministic table based on the dwelling, rather than asking the policyholder who does not know it. You can keep it or adjust it.
  • Editable recap, then confirmation; output as structured JSON.
  • No server, no model: the behaviour is predictable and replayable.
Step 2 — going further

Add AI where it genuinely helps

With the core in place, you enrich it without throwing away the schema. AI comes as a layer on top, not a replacement.

  • Understand natural-language answers ("a three-room flat, around 70 m²") and file them into the right fields.
  • Branch the flow by chosen product (home, motor, health have different fields).
  • Pre-fill from the 360 context when the client is already known.
  • Handle digressions and coverage questions via the 360 chatbot, then return to the form.

The principle

You do not wire an LLM API into everything. You start from a deterministic, verifiable core you can test and replay, then add intelligence where it brings real value, without ever losing the structure or the traceability. That holds for a subscription chatbot as for the rest of the series: reliability comes from structure, not from the model.

A journey that links several chatbots

Subscription does not live alone. While subscribing, the client asks coverage questions, and sometimes you need to estimate what they own. The same assistant can call on the other bricks, without the client switching tools.

  • Estimate the inventory. By default we offer an estimate of the contents value (the table above) so as not to bother the client. If they want to be precise, we can help them do a real, room-by-room inventory.
  • Answer coverage questions. "My bike, stolen outside my home, is it covered?" The assistant calls the sourced answer on the policy and cites the relevant guarantee (off-premises theft, personal effects) when the household policy provides for it.
  • Compare with competitors. On that precise point, the policy comparison shows which competitor contracts cover the bike stolen outside, and which do not. A concrete, sourced argument, right when subscribing.

The 360 chatbot orchestrates this back-and-forth: subscribe, answer, compare, in one conversation.
