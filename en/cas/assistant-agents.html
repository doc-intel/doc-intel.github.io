A 360 chatbot for the general agent, not just the policyholder

A general agent does not ask the same questions as a policyholder. They run a book of clients, prepare quotes, compare products. Same engine, but an assistant built for their trade, one that also surfaces insights (life events, churn, upsell, fraud, client value) the agent validates before acting.

Two 360 chatbots, two audiences. This one targets the general agent. The 360 chatbot for the client has a different tone and scope. Both sit on the same 360 context.

The problem

A general agent does not ask the same questions as a policyholder. They run a book of clients, prepare quotes, compare products, answer technical questions all day, and juggle several business tools. A reassuring, cautious consumer assistant is not what they need: they need a fast, precise working tool.

The approach

The engine is the same as the client chatbot, but the assistant is tailored to the agent's trade. The tone is professional and direct, no hand-holding. The scope is wider: several clients, several policies, access to pricing and comparison tools. The conversation is there to move a case forward, not just to answer a question.

Beyond answers: insights to validate

The agent does not only come for an answer, they come for what they do not have time to compute. Algorithms run internally on a client's context and surface insights by theme. The assistant presents them to the agent, who checks them before acting.

Why the agent first? Because these signals are sometimes delicate, and pushing them raw to the client is not wise. The agent validates a tricky insight; once it proves reliable, some alerts or messages can be pushed straight to the client. The human stays in the loop, especially when the relationship is at stake.

Life events

A move, a new pet, a birth, a property purchase… picked up in the exchanges and the file. Each one opens onto a fitting product. This signal matters on the client side too.

Churn risk

Spot a client about to leave, and offer the agent a retention gesture before they do.

Upsell & cross-sell

The next relevant product for this client, based on their profile and life events, not at random.

Fraud signals

Anomalies on a file surfaced to the agent for review, never an automatic accusation.

Client value

Why this is a good client, spelled out: tenure, multi-policy, loss ratio. Enough to adjust how they are handled.

The principle: the algorithm proposes, the agent decides. A sensitive insight is checked by a human before it reaches the client; once proven, it can be automated. That safeguard is what makes the general agent the right first recipient.

What differs from the client chatbot

The toneProfessional and concise for the agent; plain and reassuring for the policyholder.
The scopeMulti-client and multi-policy on the agent side; a single file on the client side.
The actionsQuotes, comparison, act preparation on the agent side; questions and declarations on the client side.
The accessWider on the agent side (business tools), with the authentication and traceability that go with it.

Technical vision

Same agentic orchestrator, same specialised bricks, but a richer context on the agent side: their book, their clients, their products. The assistant sits on the 360 context and on the comparison and pricing tools. On top, insight algorithms (churn scoring, life-event detection, next best product, fraud signals) run over that context and surface their results in the conversation, each traced back to its source data.

Internal API · business toolsAuthentication Like any access to a book of clients, authentication and an audit trail are essential.

Implementation roadmap

  1. Reuse the orchestratorStart from the client chatbot: intent routing is already in place.
  2. Enrich the agent contextPlug in the agent's book: their clients, their policies, their products.
  3. Add the business toolsComparison, pricing, quote preparation, as assistant actions.
  4. Authentication and traceabilitySecure access to the book and trace every look-up.

What it shows

One engine serves two different assistants. What changes is not the underlying technique, it is the audience: tone, scope, actions and access. And above all, on the agent side, the chatbot stops being a mere responder: it becomes the entry point for business insights (life events, churn, upsell, fraud, client value) that a human validates before they reach the client. Building "a 360 chatbot" without saying for whom, and who validates its recommendations, is the wrong spec.
