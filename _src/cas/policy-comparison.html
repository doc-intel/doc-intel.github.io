Comparing two homeowners policies, criterion by criterion

Two policies look alike on the brochure and diverge on the details: a deductible, a water-backup exclusion, a liability limit. A comparison aligns them on common criteria and shows exactly where they part ways.

The problem

Two home-insurance policies are easy to confuse and hard to compare. The meaning is in the limits and the exclusions, not the headline: one covers water backup, the other makes it an optional rider; one has a flat wind deductible, the other a separate one. Reading both cover to cover to answer a handful of questions is slow, and a comparison without a common frame is not a comparison.

The approach

Both policies are projected onto the same set of criteria: dwelling coverage, personal property, liability, deductible, water backup, loss of use, wind and hail. Each criterion is answered for both policies, and the cells that differ are highlighted. The comparison is a grid you read in one pass, with the divergences standing out.

The thread: a comparison is an alignment on common criteria, exclusions included. The meaning of a policy is where it differs, not where every policy agrees.

The method, step by step

Fix the criteriaA common set of coverage criteria, chosen so the two policies can actually be put side by side.
Answer for each policyEach criterion is filled for both, limits and exclusions alike.
Highlight divergencesThe cells that differ are marked, so the reader sees the choice at a glance.
Keep it verifiableOn a real document, each cell traces back to the clause it came from.

What it shows

Comparing documents is not fuzzy similarity search: it is a structured, criterion-by-criterion projection where every cell can be checked against a clause. The exclusions, easy to miss cover to cover, are exactly where the two policies separate.
