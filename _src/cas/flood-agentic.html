Agentic coverage analysis on US flood insurance

A tool-using agent reads two public FEMA flood-insurance forms, decides what to look up, and returns structured verdicts with every citation verified against the source.

The problem

Flood-insurance policies are full of traps: a coverage stated in one place, negated by an exclusion elsewhere, capped by a sub-limit three pages later. Answering "is this covered?" reliably means reading around the whole document, not grabbing the first matching paragraph. And an answer without the exact citation is worthless when money is on the line.

The approach

This is the English counterpart of the French contract-comparison study, specialized for the US National Flood Insurance Program. The agent works over two public-domain FEMA forms (the Dwelling Form and the General Property Form). It has tools, search_page and read_page, and it decides which pages to open. When it has enough context, it returns a structured verdict: coverage status, condition, exact citation, page. The citation is then checked programmatically against the source text, so a hallucinated quote cannot survive.

The point is not a bigger model. It is a bounded loop: the agent gathers evidence with tools, then commits to a verdict that must be backed by a real quote on a real page.

The method, step by step

Load the forms with page markersBoth public-domain FEMA forms are extracted with page markers so citations stay traceable.
Pick trap questionsThree coverage questions chosen to expose common failure modes: negation, exclusion elsewhere, sub-limit.
Let the agent use toolsIt searches for keywords and reads specific pages, looping until it has enough context.
Return a structured verdictStatus, condition, exact citation, page, in a typed shape.
Verify the citationEach quote is checked as a substring of the source text; unverifiable quotes are rejected.

What it shows

Agentic retrieval is not about letting a model roam free. It is a disciplined loop with tools, a typed output, and a verification gate. The same pattern that compares French home-insurance contracts transfers directly to US government forms, which is the point: the method travels, the documents change.
