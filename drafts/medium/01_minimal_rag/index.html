Context Engineering for Enterprise RAG: The Minimal Four-Brick Pipeline That Highlights Its Source

The smallest version of RAG that works, on a real PDF, with grounded answers and the source lines highlighted

Enterprise Document Intelligence [Vol.1 #1]

The naive RAG pipeline is simple. Chunk the PDF, embed every chunk, keep the closest to the question by cosine similarity, hand them to the LLM. It never works.

Why? Chunking imposes a fixed size on a document that has structure. Sometimes the answer is a single line, sometimes three pages, sometimes a table on page 8. A 500-token slice ignores all three. And embeddings? You cannot explain them to a domain expert. Why should the question and the answer be close in a 1536-dimension vector space? Why this model and not the one that came out last month?

An expert who has read the same contracts for thirty years does none of that. They know the structure of the document. They scan the right pages for two or three keywords.

This article opens the series with the smallest pipeline that respects that logic: chunk at the page level (the natural unit of the PDF), match keywords instead of embeddings, count how many of the question's keywords co-occur inside each page. Four bricks (document parsing, question parsing, retrieval, generation), an answer that comes back as typed JSON with the source lines highlighted on the page. Structure awareness comes later. About a hundred lines of Python on the Attention Is All You Need paper (arXiv non-exclusive distribution license).

Article 1 (minimal RAG) is the Part I baseline the whole series upgrades.

1. A minimal RAG: four bricks plus a PDF highlight

The pipeline has four bricks (Part II goes into each one in detail) plus a final, optional rendering step. Each brick says what it takes in and what it gives back; what we pass from one brick to the next is what we save.

The baseline RAG pipeline, end to end

A PDF and a question go in. Document parsing turns the PDF into structured rows (line_df, page_df). Question parsing turns the question into a ParsedQuestion: the normalized question plus a short list of checked keywords, and nothing else. Retrieval reads that and emits top-k page numbers, keeping the handoff small enough that the next step rebuilds the matching lines from line_df itself. Generation reads those pages and returns an AnswerWithEvidence: a typed JSON with the answer, an evidence span (page and line range), a confidence, a justification, and any caveats. PDF annotation is the optional rendering step: given the evidence span, it draws rectangles around the cited lines on the source PDF. What comes out is not a chatbot bubble. It's a sourced JSON answer plus an annotated PDF you can open and check.

Each of the four bricks gets its own article in Part II (Article 5 develops document parsing, Article 6 question parsing, Article 7 retrieval, Article 8 generation), and that is where the I/O contracts that look like throwaway choices here get earned: why question parsing holds no embedding, why retrieval hands off page numbers rather than a filtered DataFrame, why the full generation JSON is worth saving for evaluation and replay. PDF annotation is the rendering step, not a brick in itself.

The dependencies are minimal:

  • pymupdf parses PDFs into text plus position info; the bounding boxes it returns are what we use to highlight the answer back on the source page.
  • openai is the LLM client; via base_url the same library serves Azure, OpenRouter, Ollama, or any compatible endpoint.
  • pandas holds the document as a DataFrame, the format every parsing and retrieval step uses.
  • pydantic defines the answer schema that forces structured JSON with citations.

No vector database, no orchestration framework, no specialized RAG library. Later articles look at when those libraries' helpers become useful, and when they get in the way of seeing what's going on.

"For a 15-page paper, the LLM can read the whole thing. Why bother with retrieval?" Fair point on this one document. We use the paper to teach the method, not to save tokens on these 15 pages. The objection often points to the Needle in a Haystack benchmark (Kamradt, 2023), where frontier models score near-perfectly retrieving a single verbatim sentence from a 1M-token context.

That benchmark is research, not practice. A needle is one isolated, verbatim fact, while enterprise questions aggregate ("every contract whose deductible exceeds €5,000"), compare ("clause 12 across these three policies"), or summarize across many passages. None of those is a single sentence to find.

Two more practical reasons keep retrieval in the loop.

  • Enterprise documents are often long: a 300-page insurance contract, a 500-page regulatory filing, a multi-volume technical specification. Sending the whole thing to the LLM costs real money on every question, every rerun, every user, and dilutes its attention across irrelevant pages.
  • The same question runs across hundreds or thousands of documents at once: "find every contract that excludes earthquake damage", "summarize this year's regulatory changes across all filings". At that scale, "throw it all in" stops being a strategy.

Retrieval makes the pipeline survive both moves: from one short paper to one long contract, and from one document to a whole corpus.

2. The four bricks in code

Each step declares its inputs and outputs, and the steps are independent. The output of step N is the input of step N+1, saved as a named DataFrame so any step can be re-run on its own against the saved output of the previous one. In the AI-coding era, an assistant told to "fix retrieval" can quietly modify the question parser when it should have stayed untouched. Independent modules are how you work confidently on one piece without breaking the rest.

Every brick that talks to a model needs a configured client. The series uses OpenAI's Python SDK, with gpt-4.1 as the chat model and text-embedding-3-small as the embedding model, both governed by OpenAI's Terms of Use. Any provider that exposes an OpenAI-compatible endpoint (Azure OpenAI, vLLM, llama.cpp's --api-server, ...) drops in by changing base_url and the model name.

2.1 Document parsing

We extract every text line of the PDF along with its position on the page. The output is a DataFrame where each row is one line, with page_num, line_num, the text itself, and the four bounding-box coordinates x0, y0, x1, y1.

  • In: a PDF path.
  • Out: line_df (one row per text line, with page_num, line_num, text, and the bounding box) plus a page_df we'll build in section 2.3.

The bounding boxes matter: they're what we use to draw highlights on the source PDF at the end.

The parser flattens PyMuPDF's nested pages/blocks/lines into one row per line

Running line_df = fitz_pdf_to_line_df(pdf_path) on the Attention paper returns 1048 lines across 15 pages.

First five rows of line_df with page, line number, text, and bounding box

The paper, turned into rows. Each line is one row, with its text and the four numbers that locate it on the page. The x0, y0, x1, y1 columns don't mean much yet; in section 2.5 they're what we use to draw rectangles on the source PDF, exactly over the lines the model cited.

This DataFrame, line_df, is the core data structure of the rest of the series. Article 5B (the relational data model) introduces a richer relational model around it (line_df, toc_df, page_df, image_df, and more).

What this parser doesn't do: detect tables (Table 1 page 4, Table 3 page 9 flatten into plain lines), reconstruct headings, footnotes, cross-references, or handle multi-column layouts. None of this matters for the question we ask here. For other questions on the same paper, it will. Article 5 covers parsing in full.

2.2 Question parsing

Before the question goes to retrieval, we run it through a tiny LLM call. The goal is to extract the keywords most useful for searching the document: short phrases the document is likely to use, not necessarily the literal words of the question.

  • In: a text question.
  • Out: a ParsedQuestion holding the normalized question and a short list of checked keywords.

This step does not know about retrieval. It does not compute the question embedding either. That one is tied to the corpus index and lives in section 2.3. Keep that line clean and you can swap the embedding model or add a hybrid retriever tomorrow without touching question parsing.

Why bother on a minimal pipeline? Two reasons:

  • You can explain why retrieval picked what it picked. When the system answers wrong, we can see whether the keywords were off (question-parsing problem) or the right keywords landed on the wrong page (retrieval problem). Without question parsing, retrieval is a black box.
  • The question is a real input, just like the document. Section 2.1 parsed the document into line_df. This subsection parses the question into ParsedQuestionMinimal. Both inputs deserve to be parsed before they hit the search step. Article 6 (question parsing) builds the richer brick (parse_question, with answer shape, scope filters, decomposition, ...).

On the question "What are the options mentioned for positional encoding?", the call parsed_question = get_keywords_from_question(question, client=client) returns parsed_question.keywords = ['positional encoding', 'options', 'mentioned'].

The LLM produces a single, literal phrase like ['positional encoding']. That's deliberate. An earlier draft of this prompt asked for "3 to 5 short keywords useful for searching", and the LLM filled the quota with paraphrases (positional encoding options, types of positional encoding, transformer positional encoding). None of those are written in the document. Only positional encoding is. Substring matching is strict: a single missing word kills the match. The minimal version asks the LLM to do less (extract the literal noun phrase, drop the question framing) and trusts the next block to do the rest.

What this minimal version doesn't do:

  • detect an answer_shape (Q&A vs summarization)
  • decompose compound questions
  • pull from a domain glossary
  • attach retrieval hints

All covered in Article 6, under the richer parse_question brick. Here we keep two fields, corrected_question and keywords, the smallest version that makes the brick visible.

2.3 Retrieval

Retrieval cuts the document down to the pages generation will read. It takes the parsed question's keywords, scores every page against them, and returns the pages most likely to hold the answer.

  • In: the checked keywords from section 2.2.
  • Out: the top-k page numbers, plus optionally the matching line numbers within those pages.

Below we walk both retrievers side by side, so the opacity of the embedding score is visible on the same page and the choice of keyword matching as the pipeline's default is one you can check.

Why embeddings are opaque: Cosine similarity returns a number like 0.7798 and asks the user to trust that "page 6 is relevant to the question". Show that score to a domain expert, a product owner, or a manager: nobody understands what 0.78 means, or why it's higher than 0.65. Developers may argue they understand it ("dot product of normalized vectors"). They understand the math, not the relevance. Asked why this specific page scored 0.7798 against this specific question, they shrug and point at the model.

In an enterprise context, retrieval is the step users question the most. Why did the system look at this page and not that one? You have to explain it. So the minimal version uses something you can read directly: keyword matching. Section 2.2 pulled the keywords; we score each page by how many of those keywords appear in it, and keep the top three.

Where we search vs what we return: both pages here. Real retrieval has two levels. The anchor is where the keyword or embedding hits (a line, a sentence). The context is what we hand to generation (the lines around it, the page). We search small, we return big. Here we use the page for both. That works on an academic paper where each page is roughly one idea. Article 7 (retrieval) separates the two levels for long contracts, multi-column reports, table-heavy documents.

2.3.a Embeddings + cosine similarity

Embed every page (one call per page), embed the question, compute cosine similarity, keep the top-k. The output: a number like 0.7798 per page. Look at the scores below: can you tell why a page made the top three? Could you explain the ranking to a domain expert? That's the opaque-score problem the article opens with.

Top three pages by cosine similarity. Precise scores, opaque ranking

Three numbers, all very close to each other (0.7843, 0.7798, 0.7728). Can you say why page 9 beats page 6? You can't. Each of these pages probably contains content relevant to the question, but precisely why one outranks another, we cannot say. The score is opaque.

The page that answers the question, section 3.5 Positional Encoding, is page 6 and ranks last in the top three. The scores look precise, but the ranking has no story behind it: there is no token to point at, no phrase to defend, just a dot product on two black-box vectors.

This is not to say page 9 is irrelevant. It carries a Table 3 row that compares "sinusoidal positional encoding" with "learned positional embeddings", content directly related to the question, just not the section that defines them. The opacity is not that page 9 is off-topic; it is that we cannot tell, from the score alone, whether the page won because of that table row, because of vocabulary it shares with the question, or because of the average over its 149 lines. Article 7 (retrieval) develops a line-level view that makes this inspectable after the fact.

And on this question, an embedding step is not even necessary. The phrase positional encoding is the obvious key term. A naive keyword search on those two words flags page 6 (the section) and page 9 (the table row that mentions the variation) directly, with no embedding model involved. When the question carries such a specific token, the keyword route is auditable, deterministic, and finds the answer in one cheap pass. Article 7 develops the rule; the rest of this article uses keyword matching as the baseline retriever for exactly this reason.

A common counter from developers: these are intermediate results; the generation step (chat completion on the top-k) will sort the right page out anyway, so the opacity does not matter. That objection concedes the point. It admits retrieval is an obscure sub-step the downstream LLM has to clean up, and an opaque intermediate stage is exactly what an auditable enterprise pipeline cannot ship.

Embeddings work in many cases, and Article 2 (embeddings' failure modes) explains where this score comes from. But the score itself never becomes interpretable, and for the rest of this article we use a retriever you can read with your own eyes.

2.3.b Keyword matching

For each page, count how many of parsed_question.keywords appear in it (case-insensitive substring match). Drop pages with zero matches; keep the top-k by match count. The output table below carries the actual matched_keywords per page, so anyone can read it and see why a page was picked.

retrieve_pages(page_df, line_df, parsed_question.keywords, top_k=3) returns the top three pages by keyword count plus the filtered lines: 314 lines kept from pages 6, 9, 7.

Top three keyword-matched pages, with the matched terms shown per page

Three pages, ranked by match count, with the actual matches laid out. Pages 6, 8, and 9 each contain the literal phrase positional encoding; page 6 holds Section 3.5 Positional Encoding with the actual answer. Anyone reading the table can verify the result by hand: search the source for positional encoding and you'll find these three pages.

Two design choices:

  • Drop pages with zero matches: A retrieval that says "nothing matches" is more useful than one that pads with three random pages. The schema's null path (next subsection) handles the empty case cleanly.
  • We don't break ties: When pages tie at the same match count, the order is whatever pandas' nlargest returns. The downstream LLM sees the lines from all tied pages in document order and decides.

From 1048 lines to 300, and we know the right material is in there.

2.3.c A harder question: where each retriever breaks

Same pipeline, a different question. We ask about the value of epsilon used in label smoothing. The answer is on page 8 of the paper, written as ε_ls = 0.1 (Greek letter ε, never the English word epsilon). Watch what each retriever does.

Two failures of different shapes:

  • Embeddings rank pages by topical proximity. The right page (page 8, where ε_ls = 0.1 lives) may or may not be in the top three. Pages dense in math notation come up even when they're unrelated.
  • Keywords are blind to symbols. The LLM emits epsilon, label smoothing, etc. The document writes the Greek letter ε. Substring match returns zero on anything that mentions epsilon by symbol only. The page that contains the answer is invisible to the keyword retriever.

Section 4.4 picks this up as the bridge to Article 2 (Embeddings handle synonyms and surface variation) and Article 6 (richer Question Parsing pulls in alternatives like the Greek letter).

2.4 Generation

We send the retrieved lines to the LLM with the question, formatted as a tab-separated block where page_num and line_num sit next to each line. That format gives the LLM the exact coordinates it needs to cite.

  • In: the original question, line_df, and the retrieved page numbers from section 2.3.
  • Out: an AnswerWithEvidence, a structured JSON with the answer, the evidence span (start_page_num, start_line_num, end_page_num, end_line_num), a confidence, a justification, and any caveats.

The AnswerWithEvidence schema field by field, the contract the LLM has to fill

No top-level verbatim quotes field on purpose: the cited text is reconstructable from (start_page_num, start_line_num) .. (end_page_num, end_line_num) against line_df, so duplicating it would just bloat the payload. When Article 8A introduces per-item Spans, those carry a Span.quote that the validator (Article 8C) reads as the model's claim of what's verbatim and checks against the cited lines.

The raw JSON is worth saving in production: justification, caveats, and confidence all feed evaluation, audit, and replay, well beyond the answer field a chat UI shows.

We serialize the filtered lines into a TSV with header page_num line_num text, one row per line. The LLM sees the exact coordinates next to each text fragment so it can cite by (page_num, line_num) in its answer.

This makes the answer grounded: the schema forces the model to fill in (start_page, start_line, end_page, end_line), a confidence, and caveats if anything is uncertain. No prose, only a typed object with citations.

The generation call: tab-separated lines in, parsed AnswerWithEvidence out

We call answer = llm_answer_with_evidence(question, filtered_line_df, client=client) and get back an AnswerWithEvidence instance.

Three things happened that matter:

  • The answer is correct: Both options identified, paraphrased correctly.
  • The evidence span (page 6, lines 31-32) points to a specific region. Not "somewhere on page 6". Exact lines.
  • The model could not have hallucinated a citation: it only saw lines from the retrieved pages, and the schema forced a real (page, line) range we can verify.

2.5 Highlighting the source PDF

Now the satisfying part. We use the evidence span to draw rectangles directly on the source PDF.

  • In: the source PDF and the evidence span from the AnswerWithEvidence.
  • Out: an annotated PDF with rectangles drawn around the cited lines.
  • Optional. A CLI tool, a batch job, or an API may skip it; the answer with citations is already complete after section 2.4.

Three calls do the work:

  • passage_lines_df_from_answer(line_df, answer) rebuilds the cited-line DataFrame from the evidence span.
  • passage_bbox_by_page(passage_df) groups bounding boxes per page.
  • draw_passage_rectangles(pdf_path, bboxes_df, out_pdf_path) writes the annotated PDF.

PDF annotation in three steps: expand the span, union per page, draw rectangles

The middle step is the one worth looking at: bboxes_df has one row per cited page, with the union rectangle that wraps every cited line on that page.

One bounding box per cited page, wrapping every cited line on that page

With the rectangle coordinates ready, the source PDF gets rendered with the box drawn on the cited page, next to the question and the answer:

Attention paper page 6 with cited paragraph highlighted, next to question and answer

The passage is where the answer comes from. The red box wraps the Positional Encoding paragraph: the sentence that introduces the choice ("we use sine and cosine functions of different frequencies") and the two-line formula directly below it. The reader can move from the chat answer to the citation to the source paragraph without leaving the same screen. That's the whole point.

Why a box around the whole paragraph and not the exact words? Because we worked at the line granularity: line_df carries one bounding box per text line, the LLM cites a (start_line, end_line) span, and passage_bbox_by_page collapses every line in that span into one wrapping rectangle. If you want to draw the box around the exact words sin(pos / 10000^(2i/d_model)) instead of the whole paragraph, the approach is the same. Just change the granularity. Replace line_df with a word-level word_df (PyMuPDF's page.get_text("words") gives you a bounding box per word), make the schema cite (start_word, end_word), and passage_bbox_by_page already does the right thing. Same four-brick pipeline, finer scope.

4. The questions each block raises

What this minimal system does well:

  • A real, verifiable answer: A structured object with the answer, the page, the lines, the quote. The user can check the citation in seconds.
  • "Not found" handled cleanly: When the answer isn't in the retrieved lines, the schema allows null fields and the caveats field says why. No fabrication.
  • The answer linked to the source: The highlighted PDF closes the loop between the LLM's claim and the document. This is what separates a useful RAG system from a chatbot that happens to read documents.
  • Easy to follow: Each function does one thing. No hidden state, no framework magic. When something goes wrong, debugging is reading the code.

Now look at the same system again. Each block hides assumptions worth questioning.

4.1 Document parsing: we just read lines

We extracted text line by line. That's reasonable for an academic paper, but look at what we threw away: section structure, headings, table layouts, figures, footnotes, cross-references. Page 4 of this paper contains Table 1 with the per-layer complexities. We parsed each of its rows as plain lines, losing the table structure entirely. Page 9 contains Table 3, the ablation study. Same problem.

For a question like "What are the options for positional encoding?" this doesn't matter. The answer is in continuous prose. For a question like "What is the per-layer complexity of self-attention?" it suddenly does, because the answer lives in a table cell that our parser flattened into plain lines.

That's the topic of Article 5: Parsing. Documents have structure. Ignoring it is the single biggest source of downstream failure.

4.2 Question parsing: we asked for keywords, but only keywords

Our question-parsing step extracts a flat list of keywords. That works on a clean question against an academic paper. It starts to break down as soon as questions get harder.

Three things this minimal version doesn't do.

It doesn't detect intent. "Summarize chapter 3", "Translate this clause into French", "Compare X and Y" each call for a different downstream pipeline. A single keywords field can't carry that signal.

It doesn't decompose compound questions. "What are the exclusions and the deductible?" parsed as a flat keyword list pollutes the retrieval (the keywords for "exclusions" and "deductible" pull in two different scopes that interfere). Article 6 (question parsing) walks through how to detect compound questions, decide whether to decompose, and route the sub-questions independently.

It doesn't detect an expected answer shape. "What is the premium amount?" wants a number with a currency. "What are the obligations?" wants a list. "Compare the two policies" wants a table. The minimal version treats every answer as free text. Article 6 introduces the expected_answer_shape field that drives the generation template downstream.

That's the topic of Article 6: Question Parsing. The same brick, much richer JSON.

4.3 Chunking: we aggregated by page

We chose pages as the unit of retrieval. Why pages? Why not paragraphs, or sections, or fixed-size chunks of 512 tokens like every standard RAG tutorial recommends?

The answer is that page-level aggregation happens to work for this paper because pages roughly align with semantic units. On a contract, on a legal text, on a technical manual with numbered clauses, pages are arbitrary cuts and you'd want clause-level or section-level chunks instead. The "right" chunking depends on the document and the question, not on a default value.

The temptation, when a fixed-size approach starts failing, is to grid-search over chunk sizes and overlaps. That's the machine learning reflex. It's the wrong frame for what is, at its core, a structural decision. Article 3 (RAG is not ML) makes that case in full.

4.4 Retrieval: keyword matching is transparent, but blind to vocabulary

Our retrieval just worked. Page 6 came back with the matched keyword, ahead of the rest, and the Positional Encoding section is on page 6. Anyone can look at the match table and see why. That's the trade we made: the simplest possible retrieval, completely auditable.

The trade has a cost. Keyword matching is blind whenever the question's vocabulary doesn't match the document's. Three failure modes show up immediately on the same paper.

Symbol vs word: Ask "What is the value of epsilon used in label smoothing?" The keywords from question parsing are likely something like ["epsilon", "label smoothing"]. The actual answer (ε_ls = 0.1) sits on page 8, but the document writes it as the Greek letter ε, never the English word "epsilon". The substring check returns zero on the symbol-only page; only the literal phrase label smoothing lands on page 8.

Synonym mismatch: Ask "How does the model know the order of words in a sentence?" The keywords might be ["word order", "sentence order"]. The document calls this positional encoding. None of the question's keywords appear on page 6. The retriever picks pages that happen to mention "order" or "sentence" in passing, none of which contain the answer.

Paraphrase: Ask "What attention mechanism does the encoder use?" The document says self-attention and Multi-Head Attention, never the phrase "attention mechanism the encoder uses". The keywords pulled from the question, even after expansion, may or may not include the document's exact phrasing. When they do, retrieval works. When they don't, it silently degrades.

The first two failures are so common that the rest of the series spends two articles on them.

  • Article 6: Question Parsing turns the keyword extraction into a much richer step that pulls from a domain glossary, expands synonyms, and includes likely document phrasings rather than the question's literal words.
  • Article 2: Embeddings introduces vector representations that match across surface vocabulary: where embeddings shine (synonyms, paraphrase, misspellings, cross-lingual matching), where they quietly fail (negation, exact values, internal acronyms, polysemic terms), and how to combine them with keyword matching for the best of both worlds.
  • Article 7 (retrieval) and Article 9 (the upgraded pipeline) put the resulting hybrid retrieval into a real document index.

The right answer is to combine, not pick a winner. The two methods fail on almost opposite cases: embeddings stumble when the question depends on a precise symbol, named term, or exact value; keywords stumble when the asker's vocabulary doesn't appear word-for-word in the document. Running both retrievers, taking the union of their candidates, and (optionally) re-ranking with a cross-encoder is the standard hybrid recipe. Article 2 develops it; Articles 7 and 9 wire it into a corpus.

The minimal version stays single-retriever because it teaches the right reflex first: the retriever must be auditable. Keyword matching makes that reflex concrete (you can see exactly which words landed on which page). Once that reflex is in place, embeddings become a controlled addition rather than an opaque default, and combining the two becomes a deliberate engineering choice rather than a trend.

4.5 Generation: we asked for sources, and we got them

This is the block that worked best, almost too easily. We defined a Pydantic schema with start_page_num, start_line_num, end_page_num, end_line_num, confidence, justification, and caveats, and the model filled it in correctly.

How much more can we ask? A structured comparison for comparative questions, a list of conflicts if the document contradicts itself, multiple citations from multiple parts of the document, a confidence breakdown per claim. Yes to all of the above. The generation step is far more controllable than most teams realize. Article 8: Generation as Controlled Execution explores this in depth.

5. The shape of what comes next

This minimal pipeline is the spine of everything that follows. Each part of the series goes deep on one of the questions raised above.

The mistakes that kill most projects come from getting the wrong picture of one of these blocks: RAG isn't ML (Article 3), embeddings aren't magic (Article 2), not all RAG problems look the same (Article 4). That's Part I.

Each brick then gets its own deep dive: document parsing, question parsing, retrieval, generation. That's Part II, the four bricks.

Once the blocks are solid, we recombine them for cases that look like production: long documents, justification and absence handling, TOC-driven retrieval, listing questions, structured extraction, the composite pipeline. That's Part III.

Then we change scale. From one document to many. From a single paper to an archive of hundreds or thousands of documents. The architecture changes substantially. That's Part IV.

Finally, what it takes to operate the system in production: evaluation, cost and monitoring, security and compliance, the architecture of the codebase itself. That's Part V.

The blocks don't change. Their internals do.

The minimal pipeline is rung 1 of 5; every later rung upgrades the same four blocks

A few framing notes:

  • The four bricks (Part II) are the conceptual core. Most of the rest of the series is about doing each one better. Part III and Part IV are recombinations: the same four ideas at different scales and for different question types.
  • The series scope is enterprise documents. Contracts, technical specs, regulatory filings, internal procedures: all carry structure (TOC, sections, tables) and bounded vocabulary (industry jargon, expert terms). RAG works on those corpora because of that structure, not clever embedding tricks. Documents with no structure (novels, long unstructured transcripts) and questions that require intent rather than locating a passage are out of scope; Article 4 (technique-fit grid) returns to where the line falls.
  • Code is illustrative, not production-ready. What you've read works on a real PDF, but lacks the error handling, validation, caching, cost controls, monitoring, and security a production system needs. Each gets its own article.

Here's the explicit map from this minimal system to the rest of the series:

6. Conclusion

A hundred lines of Python and a Pydantic schema are enough to ship a working RAG system on a real PDF. What makes the system trustworthy is not the line count: it is the structured answer with line-level citations, the schema's null path that refuses to fabricate, and the PDF highlight that ties every claim back to its source. The four bricks (document parsing, question parsing, retrieval, generation) are the conceptual core; everything that follows in the series is about doing each one better.

The minimal version is a baseline, not a destination. The next article tackles the misconception that breaks most RAG projects: that RAG is a machine learning problem. It is not.
